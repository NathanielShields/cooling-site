The Problem of Heat

Hundreds of millions of people already face increasingly adverse climatic conditions. As wet-bulb temperatures rise, the risk moves from productivity loss to death, and existing solutions are failing the people who need them most.

1.0 Overview

The Scale of the Problem

Hundreds of millions of people already face dangerous wet-bulb temperatures across South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Arabian Gulf. The risks are not abstract: potentially lethal heat conditions are already being recorded regularly, and the economic consequences are already measurable.

Why existing solutions fail

Many of the people most exposed to extreme heat live in regions with per-capita GDPs under $5,000 USD, limiting individual and organizational resources for heat management. Additionally, all conventional cooling confines workers to air-conditioned spaces, making outdoor labor infeasible during heat events.

1.1 Human Physiology

How Heat Kills

People are ill-equipped to thrive or survive in intense heat. Animals have no natural refrigeration cycle. They rely on evaporation (the latent heat of vaporization) for cooling. The standard metric, Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT), accounts for air temperature, humidity, solar radiation, and wind: all variables driving heat transfer into and out of the body.

1.3 Middle East Focus

The Gulf: Extreme Heat as a Structural Condition

The Arabian Gulf sits at the intersection of high solar radiation, extreme humidity, and rapid urban and infrastructure expansion, making it one of the most thermally demanding operating environments on earth. Dry-bulb temperatures exceed 40¬∞C for 100 to 150 days annually across the UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman. When humidity is factored in, apparent temperatures in Qatar and the UAE reached 62¬∞C in July 2024. WBGT readings regularly breach 32¬∞C during summer working hours, the threshold at which significant physiological strain begins for workers at moderate exertion levels.

Midday Bans: A Starting Point

Regulation around midday bans reflects growing attention to heat risk and productivity loss in hot conditions. However, heat risk is not constrained to the exact timings and months covered by these bans. Studies in Saudi Arabia recorded high heat intensity between 9 AM and noon, and the region's combination of high humidity and radiative heat load means conditions can become hazardous outside the restricted windows.

1.4 Economic Argument

The Cost of Heat to Business

Labor productivity (both physical and intellectual) decreases drastically with increasing heat. The loss of even 10‚Äì15% of output across hundreds of millions of person-days represents a significant economic impact for the businesses and sectors involved.

üìâ

Productivity Collapse

At WBGT 32¬∞C, workers performing 500 W of physically demanding work (e.g. heavy lifting or operating manual tools) lose up to 50% of sustainable output. Even stationary 200 W tasks see >25% losses at these temperatures.

üí∞

Injury Costs

A single heat exhaustion workplace injury costs an employer approximately $79,000 on average (greenTEG/OSHA data), including medical, lost time, and liability, making preventive cooling economically rational.
