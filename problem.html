The Science & Scale

The Problem of Heat

Hundreds of millions of people already face increasingly adverse climatic conditions. As wet-bulb temperatures rise, the risk moves from productivity loss to death, and existing solutions are failing the people who need them most.

1.0 Overview

The Warming World

When the planet warms, even in conservative scenarios, hundreds of millions of people will face increasingly adverse climatic conditions (IPCC Sixth Assessment Report, Technical Summary, Section TS.B). The risks are not abstract: potentially deadly wet-bulb temperatures are already being recorded in South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Arabian Gulf, and the economic consequences are already measurable.

Why existing solutions fail

Many of the people most exposed to extreme heat live in regions with per-capita GDPs under $5,000 USD, limiting individual and government resources to adapt. Additionally, all conventional cooling confines workers to air-conditioned spaces ‚Äî making outdoor labor infeasible during heat events.

1.1 Human Physiology

How Heat Kills

People are ill-equipped to thrive or survive in intense heat. Animals have no natural refrigeration cycle ‚Äî they rely on evaporation (the latent heat of vaporization) for cooling. The standard metric, Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT), accounts for air temperature, humidity, solar radiation, and wind ‚Äî all variables driving heat transfer into and out of the body.

1.3 Middle East Focus

The Gulf: A Live Case Study in Workforce Heat Risk

The Arabian Gulf represents the world's most acute convergence of extreme heat and outdoor labor dependency. Temperatures exceed 40¬∞C for 100 to 150 days annually, and wet-bulb globe temperatures routinely breach the 32¬∞C danger threshold during summer working hours. Felt temperatures in Qatar and the UAE reached 62¬∞C in July 2024 when accounting for humidity. The ILO reports that 83.6% of outdoor workers in Arab states suffer from excessive heat exposure, the highest share of any world region. Yet the region hosts tens of millions of migrant workers from South and Southeast Asia performing outdoor construction, agriculture, and infrastructure work. Research by Equidem (2024) found that migrant workers in Gulf countries are routinely forced to work in WBGT conditions exceeding 45¬∞C, a level at which heat fatality risk is severe.

Midday Bans: Necessary but Insufficient

GCC countries (UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman) enforce outdoor work bans during afternoon hours from June through September. However, Human Rights Watch and the ILO consistently find that dangerous heat conditions occur well outside these restricted windows, including before noon, when bans do not apply. Studies in Saudi Arabia recorded high heat intensity between 9 AM and noon. Enforcement is also uneven: some employers deduct wages for rest breaks, and WBGT thresholds used in Qatar (32.1¬∞C) are considered too permissive by occupational health standards. HRW interviews with hundreds of workers in 2023 and 2024 documented incidents of dizziness, vomiting, fainting, and heat stroke occurring during nominally permitted morning hours.

The Underreported Death Toll

Human rights organizations estimate up to 10,000 migrant workers from South and Southeast Asia die annually in Gulf countries. Over half of these deaths are attributed to vague causes such as "natural causes" or "cardiac arrest" on death certificates, with no thorough investigation. Epidemiological evidence strongly links this excess cardiovascular mortality to occupational heat stress, as seen in the Nepalese worker dataset above. Hundreds of workers reportedly died of heat-related causes during the construction of Qatar's 2022 FIFA World Cup infrastructure. The ILO warns that with no systematic biometric monitoring in place, heat deaths are systematically misclassified across the region, making the true toll unknown.

1.4 Economic Argument

The Cost of Heat to Business

Aside from humanitarian arguments, there is a compelling private-sector case for cooling investment. Labor productivity ‚Äî both physical and intellectual ‚Äî decreases drastically with increasing WBGT. At a moderate work intensity of 300 W, the loss of even 10‚Äì15% of output on a 100-billion-person-day scale represents an economic crater.

Productivity Collapse

At WBGT 32¬∞C, workers performing 500 W of intense physical labor lose up to 50% of sustainable output. Even stationary 200 W tasks see >25% losses at these temperatures. See Figure 4 in source material.

Injury Costs

A single heat exhaustion workplace injury costs an employer approximately $79,000 on average (greenTEG/OSHA data), including medical, lost time, and liability ‚Äî making preventive cooling economically rational.
